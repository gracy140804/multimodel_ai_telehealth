ABSTRACT
The scope of the PHYTOMINE project is to design and implement a secure, modular, and computation-driven web application that digitally models the complete phytomining process, from plant growth evaluation to environmental safety assessment. The system focuses on providing accurate, validated, and traceable analysis of rare earth element recovery using plant-based mining techniques.
The project strictly operates on admin-validated input data, ensuring that all calculations across the system are performed using scientifically controlled values without assumptions or auto-generated data. This approach improves reliability, eliminates computational ambiguity, and ensures consistency throughout all modules. 
A PHYTOMINE covers five functional modules—Admin, Cultivator, Accumulator, Extractor, and Sustainer—each responsible for a specific phase of the phytomining lifecycle. The Cultivator module analyzes fern growth efficiency, the Accumulator module evaluates metal uptake and bioaccumulation, the Extractor module computes metal recovery and loss, and the Sustainer module assesses post-extraction soil safety using clustering-based environmental analysis. The system supports encrypted inter-module data exchange, ensuring confidentiality and integrity of sensitive computational outputs. 
The scope also includes role-based access control, registration approval workflows, encrypted report sharing with key-based decryption, and automated generation of a consolidated PDF report summarizing the entire phytomining process. The project is intended for academic research, sustainable mining studies, and environmental analysis simulations, and does not involve real-time field deployment or hardware integration.
 Overall, PHYTOMINE provides a scalable and secure digital framework for analyzing sustainable metal recovery processes while maintaining environmental compliance and computational accuracy.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
The rapid depletion of conventional mineral resources and the increasing environmental damage caused by traditional mining methods have created an urgent need for sustainable and eco-friendly alternatives. Phytomining has emerged as a promising solution that utilizes plants to extract valuable metals from contaminated or mineral-rich soils. This biological approach not only enables metal recovery but also contributes to soil remediation, making it a dual-purpose technique for resource recovery and environmental protection.
 PHYTOMINE is a secure, intelligent, and role-based digital platform designed to analyze, manage, and validate the entire phytomining process. The project integrates biological growth analysis, metal accumulation assessment, extraction efficiency evaluation, and environmental impact verification into a single unified system. By structuring the workflow into five dedicated modules—Admin, Cultivator, Accumulator, Extractor, and Sustainer—the platform ensures accountability, transparency, and sequential data processing at every stage. A key strength of the PHYTOMINE system lies in its admin-driven data governance, where all input values are uploaded, validated, and controlled exclusively by the administrator. This eliminates data ambiguity and ensures that all analytical outcomes are based on verified parameters. 
To further enhance data security, sensitive information and intermediate reports are encrypted, and controlled key-based decryption is enforced between modules. The project also incorporates intelligent analytical techniques to evaluate system performance and environmental safety. Biological growth parameters, metal uptake efficiency, extraction recovery, and post-mining soil safety are systematically calculated and assessed using predefined formulas and machine learning-assisted analysis. This approach ensures accuracy, consistency, and scalability of the results while reducing manual errors. 
Overall, PHYTOMINE bridges the gap between sustainable mining practices and modern computational technologies. By combining phytomining principles with secure data handling, modular role-based access, and intelligent analysis, the project provides a comprehensive framework for metal recovery while ensuring long-term environmental safety.

SYSTEM ANALYSIS
2.1 EXISTING SYSTEM
In the existing phytomining and  metal recovery practices, the analysis of plant growth, metal accumulation, extraction efficiency, and environmental impact is largely performed using manual calculations, isolated laboratory tools, or spreadsheet-based methods. These approaches lack a unified computational framework, making the overall process fragmented and difficult to validate across different stages of analysis.  Data collection in traditional systems is often decentralized and inconsistent, with values sourced from multiple experiments or assumed reference standards. As a result, there is no strict control over input validation, and calculations may vary depending on the researcher or analyst. This leads to inaccuracies, reproducibility issues, and increased dependency on human intervention. Most existing systems do not provide role-based modular access, meaning that all calculations are usually handled by a single entity or research group. There is no clear separation between growth analysis, accumulation analysis, extraction analysis, and environmental assessment, which reduces transparency and traceability in the phytomining workflow. 
 Security is another major limitation in current systems. Experimental data and calculated results are commonly stored in plain formats without encryption, making them vulnerable to unauthorized access, modification, or data leakage. Additionally, there is no secure mechanism for controlled data sharing or validation between different stakeholders involved in the process.  Environmental impact assessment in existing methods is typically performed as a standalone post-analysis, without systematic integration into the overall metal recovery workflow. This makes it difficult to accurately evaluate soil safety and sustainability in relation to the extraction process. Furthermore, report generation is mostly manual, time-consuming, and prone to human error, with no automated mechanism for generating consolidated analytical reports. 
Overall, the existing system lacks automation, security, modular structure, and integrated environmental assessment, which limits its effectiveness for scalable, reliable, and sustainable phytomining analysis.
2.1.1 DISADVANTAGES
Existing phytomining analysis systems rely heavily on manual and spreadsheet-based calculations, which increases the likelihood of computational errors and reduces result consistency across different analysis stages.
Most current systems lack a standardized data validation mechanism, making it difficult to verify the scientific accuracy and uniformity of input values used throughout the phytomining process.
The phytomining workflow is often fragmented, with plant growth analysis, metal uptake estimation, extraction efficiency, and environmental impact assessment handled as independent processes, limiting integration and traceability.
Data security and confidentiality are major concerns, as experimental data and analytical results are frequently stored in unprotected formats without encryption or access control mechanisms.
The lack of automation in report generation and result consolidation makes documentation labor-intensive and prone to human error.
Overall, existing systems suffer from limited automation, scalability, security, and standardization, making them unsuitable for large-scale, reliable, and sustainable phytomining application
2.2 PROPOSED SYSTEM
The proposed PHYTOMINE system introduces a secure, modular, and fully automated web-based platform for performing end-to-end phytomining analysis with a strong focus on accuracy, traceability, and environmental sustainability. Unlike traditional approaches, this system centralizes all input validation and computational processes, ensuring consistency and reliability across every stage of the workflow. 
In the proposed system, the Admin module acts as the single trusted source of input data. All required parameters such as biomass values, metal concentrations, extraction efficiency, and soil limits are uploaded and validated by the administrator The system is designed with a five-module architecture—Admin, Cultivator, Accumulator, Extractor, and Sustainer—where each module performs a well-defined role. Every module becomes operational only after admin approval, enforcing strict workflow sequencing and access control. This structured progression ensures that calculations are performed in the correct order and that intermediate results are properly validated before moving forward.
To enhance data security, the proposed system implements encryption-based data sharing and key-controlled decryption between modules. Sensitive values and reports are stored in encrypted form and can be accessed only through authorized keys issued by the system. This prevents unauthorized access, protects experimental data, and maintains data integrity throughout the project lifecycle.
 Each module performs automated scientific calculations relevant to its function. The Cultivator module evaluates plant growth metrics, the Accumulator module analyzes metal uptake and bioaccumulation, the Extractor module calculates recovered metal and extraction loss, and the Sustainer module assesses environmental safety using clustering-based analysis. By integrating algorithm-driven analysis, the system reduces human error and improves result consistency.Finally, the proposed system supports automated report generation, compiling results from all modules into a comprehensive PDF report. This enables easy documentation, auditability, and decision-making.
Overall, the PHYTOMINE proposed system delivers a reliable, secure, and scalable solution for sustainable phytomining analysis by combining validated inputs, modular processing, encryption, and intelligent environmental assessment.
2.2.1 ADVANTAGES:
Centralized and Validated Data Input: All critical input parameters are uploaded and verified by the Admin module, ensuring data consistency and eliminating errors caused by unverified or assumed inputs.
Modular and Structured Workflow: The system follows a well-defined five-module architecture, where each module performs a specific task in sequence, improving clarity, maintainability, and process control.
Enhanced Data Security through Encryption: Sensitive data and reports are stored in encrypted form and accessed only using authorized decryption keys, preventing unauthorized access and data leakage.
Automated Scientific Calculations: Growth analysis, metal accumulation, extraction recovery, and environmental assessment are automatically computed using predefined formulas and algorithms, reducing manual effort and human error.
Role-Based Access Control: Each user can access only their assigned module, ensuring operational discipline and preventing accidental or malicious data modification.

5.1.1 ADMIN:
The Admin Module acts as the central control unit of the PHYTOMINE platform. This module is responsible for initializing, validating, and governing the entire phytomining workflow. The admin first logs into the system and uploads all mandatory input parameters such as fern biomass values, soil REE concentrations, extraction efficiency, and soil limits. These uploaded values serve as the only source of data for all subsequent modules, ensuring accuracy and consistency throughout the system. Without admin input, no other module is permitted to function, making this module compulsory. In addition to data provisioning, the admin manages user registrations for the Cultivator, Accumulator, Extractor, and Sustainer modules by approving or rejecting requests. The admin also monitors the progress of each module by reviewing encrypted reports generate data every stage. Once all modules complete their respective analyses, the admin compiles and generates a comprehensive final PDF report, which summarizes growth performance, metal accumulation, extraction efficiency, and environmental safety outcomes.
5.1.2 CULTIVATOR
The Cultivator Module focuses onanalyzing ferngrowthperformance, which is a critical factor in phytomining success. After registering and receiving approval from the admin, the cultivator receives login credentials via email. Upon login, the cultivator can view the admin- uploaded input values, which are displayed in encrypted form to ensure data confidentiality.
By requesting a decryption key, the cultivator securely accesses the values required for computation. Using the decrypted inputs, this module calculates key biological growth parameters such as Biomass Increase, Growth Rate, and Growth Efficiency. These metrics provide insight into how effectively the plant has grown under given conditions and directly influence metal accumulation potential. After completing the calculations, the cultivator generates a report and securely logs out, passing the processed data to the next module.
5.1.3 ACCUMULATOR
The Accumulator Module evaluates the metal accumulation capability of the plant, which is the core objective of phytomining. Similar to the cultivator process, the accumulator registers, awaits admin approval, and logs in using credentials sent via email. This module receives encrypted reports from the Cultivator Module and must obtain a decryption key to access them. Once decrypted, the accumulator calculates important accumulation metrics including Total Metal Content, Uptake Percentage, and Bioaccumulation Factor (BAF). These values quantify how efficiently the plant absorbs rare earth elements from the soil. The calculated results confirm whether the phytomining process is effective.  After analysis, the accumulator generates a secured report and exits the system.
5.1.4 EXTRACTOR
The Extractor Module is responsible for evaluating the metal recovery phase of phytomining. Following registration approval and secure login, the extractor accesses encrypted accumulation reports produced by the previous module. With proper authorization and decryption keys, the extractor retrieves the necessary values for further analysis. This module calculates Recovered Metal, Extraction Loss, and Recovery Percentage, which represent the efficiency of extracting usable metal from plant biomass. These calculations ensure that accumulated metals are not merely stored in plants but are successfully converted into recoverable resources. The results are recorded in a report and securely forwarded for environmental assessment.
5.1.5 SUSTAINER
The Sustainer Module assesses the environmental impact and sustainability of the phytomining process. After admin approval and secure login, the sustainer accesses encrypted reports from earlier modules. Upon decryption, this module evaluates post-mining soil condition store ensure ecological safety. Using calculated and dataset-assisted analysis, the module determines Soil Metal Reduction Percentage, Safety Index, and Environmental Status. A clustering-based approach such as K-Means is employed to categorize soil conditions as safe or unsafe based on predefined thresholds. This ensures that phytomining does not compromise environmental health. Once the analysis is completed, the sustainer submits the final report, enabling the admin to generate the overall system report.

        "SOIL REE CONCENTRATION (mg/kg)",

        "EXTRACTION EFFICIENCY (%)",

        "GROWTH EFFICIENCY (%)",
        "TOTAL METAL (mg)",
        "UPTAKE (%)",
        "BAF",
        "RECOVERED METAL (mg)",
        "EXTRACTION LOSS (mg)",
        "RECOVERY PERCENTAGE (%)"
    ]
    target_cols = ["Reduction (%)", "Safety Index"]
    X = df[feature_cols].values
    y = df[target_cols]
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)
    # ------------------------------
    # K-Means Clustering
    # ------------------------------
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=5, random_state=42)
    clusters = kmeans.fit_predict(X_scaled)
    df["cluster"] = clusters
    # ------------------------------
    # Prepare input
    # ------------------------------
    input_data = np.array([[  
        data.initial_fern_biomass,
        data.final_fern_biomass,
        data.growth_duration,
        data.soil_ree_conc,
        data.plant_ree_conc,
        data.harvested_biomass,
        data.extraction_eff,
        data.initial_soil_ree,
        data.final_soil_ree,
        data.biomass_increase,
        data.growth_rate,
        data.growth_eff,
        data.total_metal,
        data.uptake,
        data.baf,
        data.recovered_metal,
        data.loss,
        data.recovery,
    ]])
    input_scaled = scaler.transform(input_data)
    cluster_id = kmeans.predict(input_scaled)[0]
    # ------------------------------
    # Fetch REAL values from dataset
    # ------------------------------
    cluster_data = df[df["cluster"] == cluster_id]
    reduction = cluster_data["Reduction (%)"].mean()
    safety_index = cluster_data["Safety Index"].mean()
    # ------------------------------
    # Save results
    # ------------------------------
    data.reduction = round(float(reduction), 2)
    data.safety_index = round(float(safety_index), 2)
    data.env_status = "Safe" if data.safety_index <= 3 else "Unsafe"
    data.sus_scan = True
    data.status = "SUSTAINER Analysis Completed"
    data.save()
    messages.info(request, "SUSTAINER Analysis Completed")

7.1INTRODUCTION
Software testing is a critical element of software quality assurance and represents the ultimate review of specification, design and coding. In fact, testing is the one step in the software engineering process that could be viewed as destructive rather than constructive.
7.1.1 STRATEGIC APPROACH TO SOFTWARE TESTING
The software engineering process can be viewed as a spiral. Initially system engineering defines the role of software and leads to software requirement analysis where the information domain, functions, behavior, performance, constraints and validation criteria for software are established. Moving inward along the spiral, we come to design and finally to coding. To develop computer software we spiral in along streamlines that decrease the level of abstraction on each turn.
A strategy for software testing may also be viewed in the context of the spiral. Unit testing begins at the vertex of the spiral and concentrates on each unit of the software as implemented in source code. Testing progress is done by moving outward along the spiral to integration testing, where the focus is on the design and the construction of the software architecture. Talking another turn on outward on the spiral we encounter validation testing where requirements established as part of software requirements analysis are validated against the software that has been constructed. Finally, we arrive at system testing, where the software and other system elements are tested as a whole.

7.2. TESTING PROCEDURES
Unit testing focuses verification effort on the smallest unit of software design, the module. The unit testing we have is white box oriented and some modules the steps are conducted in parallel.
1. WHITE BOX TESTING
This type of testing ensures that
All independent paths have been exercised at least once
All logical decisions have been exercised on their true and false sides
All loops are executed at their boundaries and within their operational bounds
All internal data structures have been exercised to assure their validity.
To follow the concept of white box testing we have tested each form .We have created independently to verify that Data flow is correct, All conditions are exercised to check their validity, All loops are executed on their boundaries.
2. BLACK BOX TESTING
Black Box Testing is a software testing method that evaluates the functionality of the system without examining its internal code structure or implementation logic. In the Phytomine project, this testing approach focuses on validating whether the system behaves correctly according to the specified requirements when different inputs are provided.
Objectives of Black Box Testing in Phytomine
To verify that all functional modules operate as intended.
To ensure accurate processing of soil, plant, and metal concentration data.
To validate user authentication and access control mechanisms.
To confirm correct generation of reports and analysis results.
To check system behavior under invalid or unexpected input conditions.
3. MODULE TESTING
In this part of the testing each of the conditions were tested to both true and false aspects. And all the resulting paths were tested. So that each path that may be generated on particular condition is traced to uncover any possible errors.
4. SUB SYSTEM TESTING
This type of testing selects the path of the program, according to the location of the definition and use of variables. This kind of testing was used only when some local variable were declared. The definition-use chain method was used in this type of testing. These were particularly useful in nested statements.
5. UNIT TESTING
In this type of testing all the loops are tested to all the limits possible. The following exercise was adopted for all loops:
All the loops were tested at their limits, just above them and just below them.
All the loops were skipped at least once.
For nested loop test the innermost loop first and then work outwards.
For concatenated loops the values of dependent loops were set with the help of a connected loop.
